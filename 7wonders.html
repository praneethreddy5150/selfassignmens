7 Wonders Of The World

Great Wall of China

The Great Wall of China is one of the most remarkable architectural achievements in human history, built primarily to protect Chinese states and empires from invasions by northern nomadic tribes. Construction of the wall began as early as the 7th century BC, but it was first unified and expanded under Emperor Qin Shi Huang around 221 BC after he united China. Over the centuries, several dynasties continued building and strengthening it, with the most well-preserved sections constructed during the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644). The wall stretches approximately 21,196 kilometers, making it the longest man-made structure in the world. Beyond military defense, it also helped regulate trade and transportation along the famous Silk Road. Although many believe it is visible from space, this is largely a myth. Due to its immense historical and cultural importance, the Great Wall was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1987 and remains one of the New Seven Wonders of the World.

Petra

Petra, located in southern Jordan, is an ancient city famous for its rock-cut architecture and advanced water management system, and it is one of the New Seven Wonders of the World. It was established as early as the 4th century BC by the Nabataeans, an Arab tribe who made Petra their capital and transformed it into a major trading hub connecting Arabia, Egypt, and the Mediterranean regions. The Nabataeans became wealthy by controlling important trade routes for spices, silk, and incense, and they carved magnificent structures directly into the red sandstone cliffs, which is why Petra is often called the “Rose City.” One of its most iconic monuments is Al-Khazneh (The Treasury), along with other impressive structures like Ad Deir (The Monastery), the Royal Tombs, and an ancient Roman-style theater. In 106 AD, Petra was annexed by the Roman Empire, which further developed the city, but over time, changes in trade routes and several powerful earthquakes led to its decline and eventual abandonment. Petra remained largely unknown to the Western world until it was rediscovered in 1812 by Swiss explorer Johann Ludwig Burckhardt. Today, it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (since 1985) and stands as a symbol of Jordan’s rich history and architectural brilliance.

Christ the Redeemer

Christ the Redeemer is a world-famous statue of Jesus Christ located at the summit of Mount Corcovado in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, and it is one of the New Seven Wonders of the World. The idea for the statue was first proposed in the mid-19th century, but the project officially began in the 1920s as a symbol of Christianity and peace in Brazil. Construction started in 1922 and was completed in 1931, with the statue being designed by Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa and sculpted by French artist Paul Landowski, while the face was created by Romanian sculptor Gheorghe Leonida. The monument stands approximately 30 meters (98 feet) tall, with arms stretching 28 meters (92 feet) wide, and it sits on an 8-meter pedestal, making its total height about 38 meters. It is made of reinforced concrete and covered with thousands of small triangular soapstone tiles, which were chosen for their durability and smooth finish. The statue represents Jesus with open arms, symbolizing peace, protection, and welcome to the people of Brazil and visitors from around the world. In 2007, it was officially named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, and in 2012 it was added to the list of UNESCO World Heritage Sites as part of Rio de Janeiro’s cultural landscape, remaining one of the most recognized landmarks globally.

Machu Picchu

Machu Picchu is an ancient Incan city located high in the Andes Mountains of Peru, near the city of Cusco, and it is one of the New Seven Wonders of the World. It was built in the 15th century during the reign of the Inca emperor Pachacuti and is believed to have served as a royal estate, religious site, or ceremonial center for the Inca civilization. Constructed around 1450 AD, Machu Picchu showcases the Incas’ advanced engineering skills, with precisely cut stone structures built without mortar, agricultural terraces carved into steep mountain slopes, and a sophisticated water drainage system. The city includes important structures such as the Temple of the Sun, the Intihuatana stone (a ritual stone associated with astronomy), and the Room of the Three Windows. Machu Picchu was abandoned in the 16th century, likely due to the Spanish conquest, although the Spanish never discovered the site. It remained largely unknown to the outside world until it was brought to international attention in 1911 by American historian and explorer Hiram Bingham. Today, Machu Picchu is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (since 1983) and a symbol of the architectural brilliance and cultural richness of the Inca Empire, attracting millions of visitors from around the world each year.

Chichén Itzá

Chichén Itzá is an ancient Mayan city located in the Yucatán Peninsula of Mexico and is one of the New Seven Wonders of the World. It was founded around the 6th century AD and became one of the most powerful and influential cities of the Maya civilization between the 9th and 12th centuries. The city served as a major political, economic, and religious center, showcasing the advanced knowledge of the Maya in astronomy, mathematics, and architecture. Its most famous structure is the Temple of Kukulcán (El Castillo), a massive step pyramid dedicated to the feathered serpent god Kukulcán, which is designed with precise astronomical alignment—during the spring and autumn equinoxes, sunlight creates a shadow that resembles a serpent descending the pyramid’s steps. Other significant structures include the Great Ball Court, one of the largest in Mesoamerica, the Temple of the Warriors, and the Sacred Cenote, a natural sinkhole used for religious ceremonies and offerings. Chichén Itzá shows evidence of both Mayan and Toltec cultural influences, indicating strong trade and cultural connections. The city began to decline in the 13th century for reasons that are still debated by historians, including political conflict and environmental factors. Today, it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (since 1988) and remains one of the most important and visited archaeological sites in Mexico.

Colosseum

The Colosseum, also known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, is an ancient Roman arena located in the center of Rome, Italy, and it is one of the New Seven Wonders of the World. Construction began in 70–72 AD under Emperor Vespasian of the Flavian dynasty and was completed in 80 AD by his son Titus, with later modifications made by Emperor Domitian. Built primarily from limestone, concrete, and volcanic stone, the Colosseum could hold an estimated 50,000 to 80,000 spectators, making it the largest amphitheater ever constructed in the Roman Empire. It was used for public spectacles such as gladiator contests, animal hunts (venationes), mock naval battles, executions, and dramatic performances, serving both as entertainment and as a demonstration of Roman power and engineering brilliance. The structure features a complex system of vaults, corridors, and underground chambers known as the hypogeum, where animals and gladiators were kept before contests. Over the centuries, the Colosseum suffered damage from earthquakes, stone robbers, and neglect, especially after the fall of the Western Roman Empire, but it remains a powerful symbol of ancient Rome. In 1980, it was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and today it stands as one of the most visited historical monuments in the world, representing the grandeur and cultural legacy of the Roman civilization.

Taj Mahal

The Taj Mahal is a magnificent white marble mausoleum located in Agra, India, on the banks of the Yamuna River, and it is one of the New Seven Wonders of the World. It was commissioned in 1632 by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal, who died during childbirth in 1631, and construction was completed around 1653, taking approximately 20 years and involving thousands of artisans and craftsmen from across Asia. Designed in a blend of Mughal, Persian, Islamic, and Indian architectural styles, the monument is renowned for its perfect symmetry, intricate carvings, calligraphy, and the use of precious and semi-precious stones in a technique known as pietra dura. The central dome rises about 73 meters (240 feet) high and is surrounded by four slender minarets, while the entire complex includes a mosque, a guest house, and beautifully landscaped Mughal gardens arranged in a charbagh (four-part) layout. Built primarily from white marble brought from Rajasthan, the Taj Mahal changes color slightly depending on the time of day and lighting conditions, adding to its beauty. It was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983 and remains a powerful symbol of eternal love, as well as one of the most visited and admired architectural masterpieces in the world.
